🧠Fable Analysis

A critical second look at the post ideas — what the data really says, in plain English. Reviewed 10 Jul 2026.

Your highest-converting framework: story to build, comment-gate to harvest

Two different jobs, two different winners. For reach and engagement, it's storytelling — but a specific kind: first-person, vulnerable, one real detail, and a twist the reader doesn't see coming. The dad tribute post and the "improved 23 marks but mum asked about the missing 7" video both share that DNA, and both beat generic tips by huge multiples on the same topics. But for actual conversion — leads you can contact — the winner is the comment-gate post: "Comment TOPIC and I'll send you the list" put over 150 warm parents into the DMs, which no story post has done. They're a pair, not rivals: the story builds the audience, the gate harvests it. One honest caveat — this measures engagement and lead capture, not revenue. Start noting which enrolled parents mention a post, and this question can be answered at the money level.

The book video was a paid ad

The "book that helped a kid score AL1" TikTok did well because it was boosted with money, not because it spread on its own — the raw data marks it as an ad. So its style is not proof of what works. The mum story video ("improved by 23 marks but mum asked about the missing marks") is the real winner: that one spread by itself. Copy that one.

Stop starting every post with "I almost..."

The first batch of ideas opened the same way again and again — I almost gave up, I almost cancelled, I almost deleted. Post a few of those close together and readers feel the formula, and the emotional effect dies. The updated ideas keep one or two per account and open the rest differently: a question, a parent's exact words, a scene, a surprising claim.

Never open with "Here's..."

Your worst-performing posts say things like "Here's what works" or "Here's how I do it." It sounds like a lecture before it starts. Tell the story instead — the lesson shows itself.

PAC Instagram: recycle, don't guess

Almost nobody engages on PAC Instagram yet, so there is no winning pattern there to copy — the earlier ideas pretended there was. But your dad story exploded on Facebook, and your PAC audience clearly lives there. So most PAC Instagram ideas are now repurposing jobs: turn your proven Facebook posts into carousels and reels. Cheaper, faster, and built on real proof.

LOTB Instagram: fewer comment-for-freebie posts

Half the earlier ideas asked people to comment a keyword for a freebie. Every one of those means making the freebie and replying to every comment — and the trick dulls if people see it weekly. The updated list keeps it to a few per month. The rest are posts people want to save or share on their own.

Facebook: one big story a month

The dad post landed partly because it was rare. Ten long emotional posts in a row would drain you and flatten the effect. The updated Facebook list marks which ideas are the big monthly story and fills the space between with short, light posts.

Every idea now says what it's for

Some posts build trust (stories). Some collect leads (freebies, invitations). Some prove you're good (student results, client wins). Each idea now carries a purpose tag — trust, harvest, or proof — so you can balance the month instead of posting by feel. With PSLE in September, time the proof posts for when parents are deciding.

Every idea now says how much work it is

Some ideas you can post tonight. Others need you to dig up a real story, get a parent's permission, or check in with a past student first. Each idea is tagged quick or needs prep, so on a busy day you know exactly what to grab.

What do these numbers mean?

Engagement score

The main number on every ranked post. For Instagram it's likes + comments. For TikTok it's likes + comments + shares + saves. For Facebook it's Meta's own "Interactions" total (reactions + comments + shares + saves). It's used to rank posts because it's the metric all three platforms can roughly compare on.

Rank badges (1-5)

Posts ordered by engagement score, highest first in "Top 5," lowest first in "Bottom 5." The bar length under each post is that post's engagement relative to the #1 post in its own list.

The small icons under each post

  • ❤️ Likes / reactions
  • 💬 Comments
  • 🔁 Shares / reposts
  • 🔖 Saves / bookmarks
  • 👁️ Views (video plays, or reach for Facebook)

Instagram posts only show likes, comments, and views — Instagram's own platform does not publicly expose save or share counts to anyone except the account owner inside the Instagram app itself, so the scraping tool behind this dashboard can't pull them. TikTok and Facebook both expose all five metrics.

Hashtag arrows (↑ / ↓)

How many times each hashtag appeared in that account's Top 5 posts (↑) versus Bottom 5 posts (↓). A tag with a high ↑ and 0 ↓ tends to show up on winners only — though the insights above each account usually explain that the hook matters far more than the hashtags do.

Followers

Current follower count for the account, pulled fresh each time the dashboard updates. Not shown for Facebook (that number isn't included in the manual export used for that account).
