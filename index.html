The 10 Outcomes

What tutors actually buy from PAC — the after-states. Every proof post sells one of these. Rule: the abstract line is what you must NOT write (it's coach-speak the moment you say it); the receipt line is the shape to write instead — swap in a real, consented client and real numbers, or don't run it. The reader should reach the abstract conclusion on their own.

In plain terms

The after-states are the real-life outcomes tutors are actually paying for, not "coaching," but things like: free evenings back, a full group class, feeling confident charging more, not dreading Sundays. That's the actual product. PAC itself (calls, frameworks, Notion docs) is just the vehicle to get there.

The rule has two parts. Abstract line (banned): the coach-speak version, e.g. "Tutors gain confidence and unlock scalable growth." The moment you write a sentence like that, it's dead. It sounds like every other coaching ad, proves nothing, and the reader's eyes slide past it.

Receipt line (what to write instead): a concrete, specific, provable moment. Real name (with consent), real number, real detail. Like: "Sarah filled her first group class of 8 last Tuesday, first time she's had a weekday evening free in two years." That's a receipt. It's checkable, it's specific, it could only be true.

Why it works: you never state the conclusion ("this proves tutors get their life back"). You just hand over the receipt, and the reader does the math themselves: "oh, so that's what having a full group class actually feels like." Letting them arrive at it themselves lands 10x harder than you telling them.

Enforcement: if you can't swap in a real client's real number, don't publish the post at all. A vague version with no receipt is worse than not posting.

PAC 2026: the answer is proof and personal story, not tips

Your own numbers settle the strategy question. The PAC Facebook idea bank now carries this shape: mostly trust, a strong proof bench, and exactly one harvest post, marked 'hold until earned.'

Sell the outcome, not the process

The principle behind every proof post (this is what drove the proof-of-work story format): your audience doesn't buy what you do, they buy what they become after working with you. 'Join PAC and get weekly calls, frameworks, accountability' is selling the mechanism — nobody wakes up wanting coaching calls. 'She went from trading hours at $60/hr to a group programme that did $8k in its first month' is selling the after-state, and the reader concludes 'I want that' on their own. Quick test for any draft: if the post describes what PAC includes, it's selling process — rewrite it until it shows what a tutor's life or bank account looks like after. That's why proof posts are built as receipts (a real conversation, a before/after, one true number), never as claims.

Skeptical tutors believe screenshots, not claims

PAC's audience is tutors who are skeptical of coaching. They don't believe promises — they believe specifics: a verbatim client sentence, a before/after offer rewrite, a first-month revenue number. Every proof idea in the bank now requires real, consented material (real numbers or don't run it). The daily proof-of-work story set exists precisely to generate this material — if those stories are actually posted, the proof lane of this plan is already covered.

Your highest-converting framework: the verdict

"Highest engagement" and "highest converting" are two different things — and your data actually splits them. Storytelling wins one; a completely different format wins the other. The honest answer: story to build the audience, comment-gate to harvest it. They're a pair, not rivals — the mistake would be doing only one. That's exactly why every idea on this dashboard carries a tag: TRUST ideas are the storytelling engine, HARVEST ideas are the converters.

For reach and engagement: storytelling — but a specific kind

Not "stories" in general. The pattern in your top posts is: first-person, vulnerable, with one specific detail and an unexpected twist. The dad tribute post (14,677 engagement, roughly 180x your Facebook average) and the "improved 23 marks but mum asked about the missing 7" TikTok (2,683 — your only proven organic TikTok winner) share the same DNA: a real moment, real numbers, and the emotional turn the reader doesn't see coming. The first batch of ideas opened the same way again and again — I almost gave up, I almost cancelled, I almost deleted. Your worst-performing posts say things like "Here's what works" or "Here's how I do it." It sounds like a lecture before it starts. Tell the story instead — the lesson shows itself.

What do these numbers mean?

Engagement score

The main number on every ranked post. For Instagram it's likes + comments. For TikTok it's likes + comments + shares + saves. For Facebook it's Meta's own "Interactions" total (reactions + comments + shares + saves). It's used to rank posts because it's the metric all three platforms can roughly compare on.

Rank badges (1-5)

Posts ordered by engagement score, highest first in "Top 5," lowest first in "Bottom 5." The bar length under each post is that post's engagement relative to the #1 post in its own list.

The small labels under each post

  • Likes — likes / reactions
  • Comments
  • Shares — shares / reposts
  • Saves — saves / bookmarks
  • Views — video plays, or reach for Facebook

Instagram posts only show likes, comments, and views — Instagram's own platform does not publicly expose save or share counts to anyone except the account owner inside the Instagram app itself, so the scraping tool behind this dashboard can't pull them. TikTok and Facebook both expose all five metrics.

Hashtag arrows (↑ / ↓)

How many times each hashtag appeared in that account's Top 5 posts (↑) versus Bottom 5 posts (↓). A tag with a high ↑ and 0 ↓ tends to show up on winners only — though the insights above each account usually explain that the hook matters far more than the hashtags do.

Followers

Current follower count for the account, pulled fresh each time the dashboard updates. Not shown for Facebook (that number isn't included in the manual export used for that account).
